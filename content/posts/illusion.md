## THE THESIS (one sentence)

Experience is not practice. "Learning on the job" is mostly maintenance,
not improvement. Real improvement comes from deliberate drills — and the
reason knowledge work seems un-drillable isn't that it is, it's that its
FEEDBACK LOOP is broken. So:

    A drill for knowledge work is anything that manufactures a tight
    feedback loop around a sub-skill you're avoiding.

That reframe is the "so what": it turns "here's a random list of candidate
drills" into a principle with a mechanism. Lead intellectual (experience
≠ improvement), land emotional (do you actually still want to get better,
or have you quietly decided you've peaked?).

The honest, defensible claim (don't overclaim past this):
Feedback is the bottleneck, so the highest-leverage move is engineering
feedback loops into work that doesn't have them. NOT "I cracked how to
master knowledge work."


## THE TWO-LAYER MODEL (added after Hany's reply — see correspondence below)

Hany's answer revealed the thesis was missing a layer. Don't force-fit
everything into "feedback loops"; the work splits in two:

  LAYER 1 — EXECUTION: getting better at a problem you're already on.
  This is where the feedback-loop drills live (Emily catching sloppy
  thinking, explaining ideas to outsiders, coding it yourself). Our
  original thesis owns this layer completely.

  LAYER 2 — PROBLEM-FINDING: which problem to even point at. The three
  questions (better world / my skills / interesting for years), "notice an
  inconsistency and pull the thread," studying adjacent fields. NONE of
  these are feedback loops — there's no answer key for "is this the right
  problem" for years. This layer is about CONTACT RATE / exposure, not
  error-correction.

The bridge between layers is SELF-DOUBT. When there's no coach and no
answer key, the only fast feedback is the one you manufacture by
interrogating yourself. Emily = external corrector; self-questioning =
internalized corrector. That completes the arc:

  coach (beginner) → reality / adversary (practitioner)
                   → self-interrogation + one trusted human (frontier)

Same move every time — FORCE THE FEEDBACK — only the source changes as you
run out of people who can correct you. Hany's sharpest line is the Layer-2
thesis: "innovation is... developing habits that put you in contact with
interesting problems."


## THE ARC (scene-driven, like Prospector's Trap / Sunk Cost)

1. OPEN ON A SCENE — me, 32, in a ski/climbing lesson, breakthrough on
   something I'd done "fine" for years. The specific joy of it.
   Turn: when's the last time anyone watched you do your actual job and
   corrected your form?

2. THE ASYMMETRY — we drill physical skills forever (tennis serve, scales,
   ski turns); we train ~20 years for a career then declare ourselves done
   and "learn on the job." Name the ILLUSION OF MASTERY: competence is
   exactly what makes you stop training.

3. THE MECHANISM — it's not coaching, it's the drills underneath it.
   Experience ≠ improvement (evidence below). So what's a drill for
   knowledge work? The unlocking idea: the binding constraint isn't reps,
   it's FEEDBACK. Skiing gives instant feedback (you stick the line or you
   don't); a desk gives you none for years. A drill = manufacture the
   feedback loop. Explore honest candidates as INSTANCES of that principle,
   not a random list.

4. THE FRONTIER TURN — Hany Farid (uncle, world deepfake expert). REPLY IN

   frontier the execution drills still run on feedback (the driveway he
   wished someone would grade; Emily as forcing function; coding it himself

   finding, which has no feedback for years and runs on exposure habits

5. LAND ON THE REAL QUESTION — do you want to get better? Most people

   is ahead. The dread inverts into the premise. Honest non-resolution: luck
   is real and taste doesn't erase it; the bet is on the habits, not on
   certainty. coach → reality → self-interrogation: same move every time.


## EVIDENCE — "data X and anecdote Y" (all verified, links are primary)

DATA — experience only becomes expertise where feedback is fast:

- Hogarth's "kind vs. wicked" learning environments. Kind = stable rules,
  fast accurate feedback (chess, golf); experience reliably improves you.
  Wicked = feedback poor, delayed, or missing; experience does NOT improve
  you (and can entrench bad habits). This is the spine of the argument.
  > Hogarth, Lejarraga & Soyer (2015), "The Two Settings of Kind and Wicked
  > Learning Environments," Current Directions in Psychological Science.
  > https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721415591878
  > Accessible popularization (David Epstein, also good to link):
  > https://davidepstein.substack.com/p/kind-and-wicked-learning-environments

- Kahneman & Klein: skilled intuition requires a HIGH-VALIDITY environment
  + repeated practice WITH immediate feedback. Where those conditions fail,
  expert confidence detaches from expert accuracy. (Why the 20-yr veteran
  isn't reliably better than the 5-yr one.)
  > Kahneman & Klein (2009), "Conditions for Intuitive Expertise: A Failure
  > to Disagree," American Psychologist 64(6), 515–526.
  > https://doi.org/10.1037/a0016755

ANECDOTE / MECHANISM — drills work by isolating a weakness + supplying an
answer key:

- Chess: serious solitary STUDY (positions/puzzles, immediate known answer)
  predicts rating far better than tournament PLAY. "Playing games" =
  experience; "drilling puzzles" = deliberate practice. Direct parallel to
  "doing your job" vs "the thing you're not doing." Charness found study was
  the strongest predictor; grandmasters logged ~5000 hrs of study in their
  first decade, ~5x intermediates.
  > Charness et al. (2005), "The role of deliberate practice in chess
  > expertise," Applied Cognitive Psychology.
  > https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/acp.1106
  > Open PDF: https://clinica.ispa.pt/sites/default/files/11_-_the_role_of_dp_in_chess_expertise.pdf

- Franklin's writing drill (the cleanest "drill for pure knowledge work"
  anecdote): took Spectator essays, jotted terse notes on each point, then
  days later reconstructed the full essay from only his notes and DIFFED it
  against the original to find his faults. Isolate sub-skill + manufacture
  an answer key. His words: "I compared my Spectator with the original,
  discovered some of my faults, and corrected them."
  > Franklin, Autobiography (primary text, Project Gutenberg #148):
  > https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/148

- (Optional 4th, VERIFY BEFORE CITING) diagnostic radiology as the
  canonical no-feedback expert job — was there actually cancer? you find out
  months later or never — trained via decks of already-solved cases with the
  answer on the back. A flashcard drill for expert cognition. Treat as
  illustrative until the specific study is nailed down.

- Stockdale Paradox (for the LAND beat, via Hany): "never confuse faith that
  you will prevail... with the discipline to confront the most brutal facts."
  Admiral James Stockdale, via Jim Collins, Good to Great (2001), ch. 4.
  Verified quote + concept.
  > https://www.jimcollins.com/concepts/Stockdale-Concept.html


## THE CAVEAT TO KEEP (so a technical reader trusts it)

Don't claim drills TRANSFER wholesale to "being a better founder/leader" —
the research genuinely doesn't support broad transfer, and Ericsson himself
was skeptical about ill-structured domains. The narrow, strong claim:
feedback is the bottleneck → engineer feedback loops into feedback-poor
work. "Worth trying because of X and Y," not "I solved it."


## CORRESPONDENCE WITH HANY

--- Round 1, his reply (verbatim — primary source for the frontier section) ---

"This is a really thoughtful question and the fact that you are simply asking
it is probably half the answer you are looking for.

Just today we graded our driveway for the first time and I told Emily that I
would really like for someone to come look at it and tell me what I did
wrong. So I think you are right; repetition alone is necessary but not
sufficient.

In my career, more often than not there was no clear North Star. We didn't
really know what the problems were at the time, how to solve them or even if
we could solve them, and if anybody would care either way. In this world of
uncertainty, there are a few things that have guided me. I continually found
myself asking 'will solving this problem make the world a better place, do I
have the skills to solve this problem, and is it a cool/interesting problem
to spend years exploring?'

This is the big picture thinking, but there is small picture thinking too,
analogous to your skiing drills. For me they are things like: explaining a
hard idea simply to people outside of my field; studying areas well outside
of my expertise, but close enough that I can make connections and draw
inspiration; obsessively coding up every idea myself (and not outsourcing it
to students); always, always, doubting and questioning myself to make sure
I'm not letting important new ideas slip through the cracks — this gets
harder as you get older and more set in your ways, but Emily is an incredible
forcing function because she just won't let me get away with sloppy thinking;
and lastly, understand that failure is part of the process; for every idea
that worked out in my career, there were probably 99 that didn't.

Innovation, in my experience, is less about having a grand vision fully
formed and more about developing habits that put you in contact with
interesting problems. You notice an inconsistency, a weakness in an argument,
a technical limitation, or a mismatch between what people believe and what
the evidence says. Then you start pulling on that thread and don't let go.

Like I said above, just the fact that you are asking this question means you
are already on the right path. Keep asking the question and don't get
discouraged if the answer won't always be obvious. It certainly wasn't for
me."

Pull-quotes worth using: the driveway; "repetition alone is necessary but
not sufficient"; "developing habits that put you in contact with interesting
problems"; "pulling on that thread and don't let go"; the 99:1 failure ratio.

--- Round 2, his reply (verbatim) ---

"Early on in my career I worked on several different areas and only really
converged on the digital forensics work after 5+ years when it became more
clear that the problems were interesting, tractable, and useful. A lot of
what you are trained to do during PhD/Postdoctoral work is to learn how to
sniff out interesting problems. This is probably one of the hardest things to
learn because there is no fixed set of rules or criteria. This, sadly, is
less true today because graduate work has changed and become fiercely
competitive and grad students, for the most part, only want to publish papers
which can be at odds with deeper exploration and training.

One thing I've always kept in mind is the Stockdale principle (Stockdale was
a prisoner of war with John McCain): 'You must never confuse faith that you
will prevail in the end—which you can never afford to lose—with the
discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality,
whatever they might be.' This balance between faith and brutal facts can be
hard to balance and errors on either side can be costly. But both are
important: faith that you can solve hard problems, but also brutal honesty
when things aren't working and pivoting when the time calls for it.

Maybe you got lucky with your startup, maybe you didn't. We can't deny that
luck (both good and bad) plays a big part in our lives. We should have the
humility to recognize that and the courage to keep going knowing that not
everything is in our control. But you are early in your career with a lot
more ahead of you than behind. I tell my graduate students to not worry about
their PhD dissertation — if they do it right, it will be the least
interesting/exciting work they do in their careers because they know the
least and they will (I assume) only get better and smarter over the years.
You can think of your startup in the same way, it (hopefully) will be the
least interesting/exciting thing you do in your career.

I like that you are thinking about these deep and important questions. Learn
from this past stage. What did you do well? Do more of that. What did you not
do well? Do less of that. But don't worry, there is plenty of time to make a
whole new set of mistakes (I certainly have and still do). That is ok. What
matters is how you learn and grow from them."

WHAT ROUND 2 SETTLES:
- Problem selection = CONVERGENCE, not upfront picking. 5+ years of several
  areas before forensics "became clear." Confirms the portfolio/draws reframe:
  he didn't pick the winner, he took swings and converged on
  interesting+tractable+useful (= his 3 round-1 questions). Answers Sam's
  "how did taste get built": by doing, over years, with brutal honesty.
- Problem-sniffing is explicitly TRAINED (PhD/postdoc) but ill-structured —
  "no fixed set of rules." Ties straight back to Hogarth (wicked) and to the
  Layer-2 thesis. Footnote candidate: modern grad school optimizes a fast
  proxy (publications) that crowds out the slow real skill (taste) — a
  feedback-loop pathology, the mirror image of our whole argument.
- STOCKDALE PARADOX is the answer to the "looming dread": the cure isn't
  certainty, it's holding faith + brutal facts at once, and pivoting on the
  facts. Sam already did this — adapting through the industry changes WAS the
  Stockdale discipline. (Verified citation in sources below.)
- LUCK: he refuses to resolve whether it was luck — says that's the wrong
  frame. Humility it plays a role + courage to keep going. Preserve this
  non-resolution; don't let the post pretend taste eliminates luck.
- THE REFRAME THAT DISSOLVES THE DREAD: "your startup will hopefully be the
  least interesting thing you do." The dread assumes the startup was a PEAK;
  reframe it as the FLOOR. This is the emotional landing AND it resolves the
  title — see below.


## SAM'S PERSONAL STAKE (candidate personal anchor — this is the post's "why now")

From the round-2 reply: reflecting on the startup journey, Sam now believes
the original idea had a low ceiling of upside. Scraped into an acquisition by
adapting through industry upheaval. Pleased with the outcome, wouldn't change
it — but enough LUCK involved that he wouldn't want to depend on it again.
The feeling to avoid: "looming dread that there's almost no way to make it
work." Open question he's living: is there a better way to pick the next
thing than there was at 27, or do you just learn to sit with the uncertainty?

This is the emotional engine for the LAND beat (#5). It also reframes the
whole post: the problem-finding layer isn't abstract — it's "how do I pick a
next thing with a real ceiling instead of getting rescued by luck again."
Note the honest tension: maybe taste lowers the variance, OR maybe frontier
work is irreducibly luck-soaked (Hany's 99:1) and the skill is tolerating
that. Don't resolve it falsely.
